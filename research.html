Research interests

The divergence of economic history from economics has impoverished our ability to explain the modern world. My research shows how the two fields can be combined.

In terms of historiography, I contribute to the global history of the interwar economy, the business and financial history of the Netherlands since 1815, and the economic and social history of pre-partition Ireland.

My completed and ongoing projects address banking crises, monetary policy, cooperative organisations, bankruptcy and liquidation, famines and anthropometrics, language economics, and the design of patent systems.

Dutch interwar financial history

My main research contribution to date has been to Dutch financial history. I have explored the causes, anatomy and consequences of the country’s financial crisis in the 1920s, and the costs of the policy choices made during this period for the 1930s.

I combine corporate financing data with archival evidence to explain the behaviour of households, business leaders and policymakers. This research examines shareholder liability and risk-taking, religious networks and bank performance, bank balance sheets, cooperative banking, conflicts of interest, and the constraints placed on policy during the Depression.
