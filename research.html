Research interests

My research combines economics and history to examine how institutions and policy shape economic behaviour and long-run development. It currently falls into four broad areas.

Dutch interwar financial history

My research on the Netherlands examines the causes and consequences of the financial crisis of the 1920s and the policy choices it shaped during the Depression. Combining corporate finance data with archival evidence, I have studied bank risk-taking, how the country’s socioreligious pillarisation shaped the industrial organisation of banking, the resilience of cooperative banks, and conflicts of interest within universal banks. I have also examined how memories of the crisis constrained monetary policymaking during the 1930s. More recently, my work has turned to bankruptcies and liquidations among non-financial corporations during this period.

Irish nineteenth-century economic history

Much of my current research examines how households and institutions responded to the profound changes that reshaped nineteenth-century Ireland. I have studied the demographic and anthropometric consequences of the Great Famine. I am currently working on two projects: one examines the decline of the Irish language, while the other explores Ireland’s uneven economic development within the United Kingdom.

Patent institutions in historical perspective

I study how patent systems shaped innovation, technology transfer, and access to finance. My work examines distinct episodes in patent history to reveal how the design and administration of patent institutions affected their economic role. One project studies the highly discretionary patent regime of the United Kingdom of the Netherlands from 1817, focusing on access to patents and technology transfer. Another examines the introduction of patent examination in Britain in 1905 and asks how it changed the information conveyed by patents and whether innovative firms could use them to raise external finance.

Applied economic history

I am interested in how historical research can inform policy without reducing the past to a collection of simple lessons. My work examines how policymakers use historical narratives and analogies, how disciplined comparison can distinguish useful precedents from misleading ones, and what we can learn from teaching economic history to government economists. It also informs my efforts to bring economic history back into the economics curriculum.
