About me

I am a Reader in Economic History in the Department of Economics at Queen’s Business School (QBS), Queen’s University Belfast, and Co-Director of the university’s Centre for Economic History (QUCEH).

I also serve as Associate Director for Outreach and Engagement at the Centre for Economics, Policy and History (CEPH), a Government of Ireland-funded Centre of Excellence linking Queen’s with Trinity College Dublin.

As an Associate Editor at Business History, I have particular responsibility for submissions that employ quantitative methods.

Aside from my research, I conduct pedagogical scholarship on ways to better integrate economic history into the economics curriculum. My edited book, An Economist’s Guide to Economic History (2018), provides accessible historical material that can be incorporated into undergraduate teaching.

Research news

Mind Your Language!

With Alan Fernihough, Eoin McLaughlin, and Philip Neary, I investigate why Irish fell into disuse in the nineteenth century using network game theory and micro-census data.

Read the paper (May 2026 update)

Applied Economic History

With Johan Fourie, I examine how historical narratives and disciplined comparison can inform policy. With Andrew Dorman, David Jordan, and Duncan Needham, I study how applied economic history operates in practice.

Read the first (May 2026 update) · Read the second (QUCEH working paper 26-02)

Patent Examination and Finance

With Stephen Billington and Christopher Coyle, I explore how the introduction of patent examination in the UK in 1905 affected innovative firms’ access to bond markets.

Read the paper (CAGE working paper 767/2025) · Listen to my CEPH podcast interview

Patently Peculiar

With Homer Wagenaar, I examine the unusually discretionary patent system of the United Kingdom of the Netherlands.

Read the paper (QUCEH working paper 25-04) · Listen to my CEPH podcast interview

One Century of Terminations

With Abe de Jong, Philip Fliers, and Florian Madertoner, I investigate a century of Dutch corporate terminations to understand why publicly listed corporations fail.

Read the article (Business History 2026) · Watch the video abstract

Scarring and Selection

With Matthias Blum and Eoin McLaughlin, I compare cohorts born during the Great Irish Famine with those born immediately before and after, finding selection in the countryside and scarring in the city.
